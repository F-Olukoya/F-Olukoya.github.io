8  Groups and Numbers

The aim of this chapter is to look at the relationship between group theory and number theory. We begin by revisiting some basic ideas that we met in first-year Algebra before considering what happens when we examine the set \(\zn\) under multiplication rather than addition. This leads to a consideration of what we call the Reduced Residue System. We then use the results of that analysis to shed light on some problems in number theory.

8.1 Basic Concepts

We first state, as a reminder, some results from Algebra that we shall be using in this chapter (though the proofs are omitted and will not be required).

  1. Let \(d, n \in \Z\), then \(d\) divides \(n\) if and only if \(n\) is an integer multiple of \(d\), that is, \(d \divides n\) if and only if \(\exists \, q \in \Z\) such that \(n=qd\).

  2. If \(d \divides m\) and \(d \divides n\), then \(\forall \, a, b \in \Z\), we have that \(d \divides (am+bn)\).

  3. Let \(d, n \in \Z, \, d>0\). Then \(\exists\) unique \(q, r \in \Z\) such that \(n=qd+r, \, 0\leq r<d\).

  4. Let \(a=qb+r\), where \(a, b, q, r\) are all integers. Then \(d\) is a common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\) if and only if \(d\) is a common divisor of \(b\) and \(r\).

  5. Let \(a=qb+r\), where \(a, b, q, r\) are all integers. Then \(\greatcd (a, b)= \greatcd (b, r)\). This is known as Euclid’s algorithm.

  6. Let \(a, b, m\) be integers, \(m>0\). Then \(a\) is congruent to \(b\) modulo \(m\) if and only if \(m \divides (b-a)\). We denote this \(a \equiv b \modu m)\).

  7. If \(a \equiv \ap \modu m)\) and \(b \equiv \bp \modu m)\), then

    1. \(a+b \equiv \ap + \bp \modu m)\),
    2. \(ab \equiv \ap \bp \modu m)\).

This last property means that the result obtained from doing the arithmetic first and then reducing modulo \(m\) is the same as the result obtained by reducing all of the numbers modulo \(m\) first and then doing the arithmetic.

Example 8.1  

Consider: \[14 \times 7 \pmod{6} = 98 \pmod{6} \equiv 2 \pmod{6}.\] However \(14 \equiv 2 \pmod{6}\) and \(7 \equiv 1 \pmod{6}\), therefore \[14 \times 7 \equiv 2 \times 1 \pmod{6} \equiv 2 \pmod{6}.\]

Euclid’s algorithm does two things of interest; it finds \(\greatcd (a, b)\) and it enables us to express this greatest common divisor in the form \((ma+nb)\) where \(m\) and \(n\) are integers.

Example 8.2 Find \(\greatcd (207, 567)\) and then find integers \(s\) and \(t\) such that \[\greatcd (207, 567) = 207s+567t.\]

Set \(a = 567\) and \(b=207\), then:

\[\begin{array}{r|l} a = 567 & \\ \underline{\phantom{blahhhhh}2b=414} & b = 207 \\ {a- 2b =153} & \underline{a - 2b = 153\phantom{mm}} \\ \underline{\phantom{h}-2a + 6b = 108} & -a + 3b = 54 \\ 3a - 8b = 45 & \underline{3a - 8b = 45\phantom{mm}} \\ & -4a + 11b = 9 \end{array}\]

Thus the greatest common divisor if 207 and 567 is 9; \(s=11\) and \(t = -4\).

An important property of \(\greatcd (a, b)\) is that it is the smallest positive integer which can be expressed in the form \(ma+nb\); this result is a corollary to the following theorem.

Theorem 8.1 Let \(a\) and \(b\) be non-zero integers. Then, \[\{ma+nb \where m, n \in \Z\} = \{k \times \greatcd (a, b) \where k \in \Z \}.\]

Proof. Let \(S = \{ma+nb \where m, n \in \Z\}\) and \(T = \{k \times \greatcd (a, b) \where k \in \Z \}.\)

Let \(d = \gcd(a,b)\).

We first show that \(T \subseteq S\). We can find \(u,v \in \Z\) such that \(d = ua + vb\). Thus for any \(k \in \Z\), \(kd = (ku)a + (kv)b \in S\).

Let \(m,n \in \Z\). Since \(d\) divides both \(a\) and \(b\), there are \(\alpha, \beta \in \Z\) such that \(a = d \alpha\) and \(b = d \beta\). It follows that \[ma + nb = (m\alpha) d + (n\beta) b = (m\alpha + n \beta)d \in T.\] Therefore \(S \subseteq T\).

We conclude that \(S =T\).

Corollary 8.1 Let \(a, b \in \Z\), \(S = \{ma+nb \where m, n \in \Z\}\). Then \(\greatcd (a, b)\) is the smallest positive integer in \(S\).

Proof.

Let \(d = \gcd(a,b)\). By Theorem 8.1 \(S = \{kd: k \in \Z\}\). Clearly \(d\) is the smallest positive integer in \(\{kd: k \in \Z\}\) and so \(d\) is the smallest positive integer in \(S\).

We have already been using the following concept, but we repeat the formal definition.

Definition 8.1 (Relatively Prime) Two integers \(a\) and \(b\) are relatively prime if and only if \(\greatcd (a, b) = 1\).

Pairs of integers which are relatively prime include (7, 11), (8, 9), (12, 55), (-14, 65). Pairs of numbers which are not relatively prime include (6, 6), (15, 126), (-4, 18). When asked to prove something about relatively prime numbers we often use the following fact.

Corollary 8.2 Two integers \(a\) and \(b\) are relatively prime if and only if there exist integers \(m\) and \(n\) such that \(ma+nb=1\).

Proof.

If \(\gcd(a,b) = 1\), then we can find integers \(m,n \in \Z\) such that \(ma + nb =1\).

On the other hand, \(d = \gcd(a,b)\) is the smallest positive integer in the set \(S=\{ma +nb : m,n \in \Z\}\). If \(1 \in S\), then it is clear that \(d = 1\).

Corollary 8.3 If \(d \divides ab\) and \(d\) and \(a\) are relatively prime, then \(d \divides b\).

Proof.

We can find \(m,n \in \Z\) such that \(1 = md + na\). It follows that \[b = b(md + na) = bmd + bna.\] Since \(d|ab\) and \(d|d\) we conclude that \(d| (bmd + abn)\). Therefore \(d|b\) as required.

Example 8.3 The numbers \(7\) and \(5\) are relatively prime hence if \(7\) divides \(5n\) for \(n \in \Z\), then \(7|n\).

Now consider \(6\) and \(15\). We have \(\greatcd(6,15) = 3\) and so \(6\) and \(15\) are not relatively prime. Observe that \(6|30\) but \(6\) divides neither \(2\) nor \(15\).

Of fundamental importance in number theory is the class of numbers called prime numbers.

Definition 8.2 (Prime Number) A positive integer \(p\) is prime if and only if \(p>1\) and the only positive divisors of \(p\) are \(p\) and 1.

We refer to any positive integer that is not prime as composite. It is worth noting that if \(p\) is prime and \(a\) is any integer then the statement ‘\(p\) does not divide \(a\)’ is equivalent to saying that \(p\) and \(a\) are relatively prime (that is, \(\gcd (p, a)=1\)). This is not the case if \(p\) is not prime, for example 10 does not divide 18 but 10 and 18 are not relatively prime. The importance of the class of primes is a consequence of the following theorem which we state without proof.

Theorem 8.2 (Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic) Let \(n\) be a natural number. If \(n \geq 2\) then \(n\) is uniquely expressible as a product of primes (except for reordering).

Proof. Omitted.

Example 8.4 Some examples

  • \(42= 2 \times3 \times 7\)
  • \(64 = 2^6\)
  • \(100 = 2^2 \times 5^2\)
  • \(101\) is prime.

The uniqueness property of a prime decomposition may seem obvious, but it depends on the number system. Of course who would have to first make precise what the definition of a ‘prime’ is in the new number system. Consider the following example.

Example 8.5 Consider the even integers \(2\Z\). What are the primes? We consider a positive integer \(m \in 2\Z\) prime if it is a not composite. Using this definition, the primes in \(2\Z\) are \[\{2(2k+1): k \ge 0\} = \{2,6,10,14 \ldots \}.\]

It is clear that any element of \(2\Z\) can be factorised as a product of primes. Indeed any element of \(2\Z\) which is not prime is of the form \(2^i m\) for some \(m \in \{2(2k+1): k \ge 0\} = \{2,6,10,14 \ldots \}.\)

Do we have unique factorisation into primes? Take the number \(36\). This can be factorised either as \(2 \times 18\) (both of which are prime with our definition) or as \(6 \times 6\). Therefore we do not have unique factorisation.

Perhaps we have the wrong definition. What if we stick with Definition 8.2 unchanged. With this definition of prime, the only element of \(2\Z\) that is prime is \(2\). In this case we do not always have a factorisation into primes! Indeed any even integer that is not a power of \(2\) cannot be factorised as a product of primes in \(2\Z\).

Theorem 8.3 Let \(a, b, m \in \Z\). Then \(\greatcd (a, m) = 1\) and \(\greatcd (b, m) = 1\) if and only if \(\greatcd (ab, m) = 1\).

Proof.

Suppose \(\gcd(a,m) = \gcd(b,m) = 1\). There are numbers \(u,v,s,t \in \Z\) such that \(ua + vm= sb + tm =1\). it now follows that \((ua +vm)(sb + tm) =1\). Expanding: \[(ua +vm)(sb+tm) = (as)ab + m(atu + bsv + mtv) = 1.\] It follows that \(1\) is the smallest positive integer in \(\{j(ab) + km : j,k \in \Z\}\). Therefore \(\gcd(ab,m) = 1\) as required.

On the other hand suppose that \(\gcd(ab,m) = 1\). Then \(1\) is the smallest positive integer in \(\{j(ab) + km : j,k \in \Z\}\). However, \(\{j(ab) + km : j,k \in \Z\} \subseteq \{j'a + k'm : j',k' \in \Z\}\) and \(\{j(ab) + km : j,k \in \Z\} \subseteq \{j'b + k'm : j',k' \in \Z\}\) . Therefore \(1\) is the smallest positive integer in each of \(\{j'a + k'm : j',k' \in \Z\}\) and \(\{j'b + k'm : j',k' \in \Z\}\). Therefore \(\gcd(a,m) = \gcd(b,m) = 1\) as required.

8.2 Reduced Residue System

In the Algebra module we explored the idea of congruence modulo some positive integer \(n\). It is worth reminding ourselves of the definition:

Definition 8.3 (Congruence) Let \(n \in \N\) and let \(a,b \in \Z\). We say \(a\) is congruent to \(b\) modulo \(n\), and write \(a \equiv b \pmod{n}\), to mean that \(n \divides (a-b)\).

Example 8.6  

  • \(15 \equiv 1 \pmod{7}\) since \(7| (15-1)\);
  • For any integer \(x\) and any positive integer \(n\), \(x \equiv x \pmod{n}\) as \(n | (x-x)\).

It should be clear that for some given \(n\) there will be an infinite number of integers congruent to some fixed \(a\) modulo \(n\) and that we can group those integers together in a set. This is best illustrated by example.

Example 8.7  

Let \(a = 2\) and \(n=5\). Then:

\[\begin{eqnarray*} 2 &\equiv & 2 \pmod{5} \\ 7 &\equiv & 2 \pmod{5} \\ 12 &\equiv & 2 \pmod{5} \\ -3 &\equiv & 2 \pmod{5}. \end{eqnarray*}\]

This leads to the following definition.

Definition 8.4 (Residue Class) Let \(a, n\) be integers, \(n\) positive. The residue class of \(a\) modulo \(n\) is denoted and defined by \[[a]_n = \{ m \where m \in \Z, m \equiv a \modu n) \} = \{a+kn \where k \in \Z \}.\]

Example 8.8  

\[\begin{eqnarray*} [1]_{5} &=& \{\ldots, -14,-9,-4,1,6,11,\ldots\} \\ {[3]}_{7} &=& \{\ldots, -11,-4,3,10,17,\ldots \} \\ {[0]}_{4} &=& \{\ldots, -12,-8,-4,0,4,8,\ldots\}. \end{eqnarray*}\]

Aside

Consider \(H = \{ 5k : k \in \Z\}\). This is a subgroup of \((\Z,+)\). Notice that the left coset of \(H\) containing \(1\) is precisely the left coset

\[1H= \{ 1 + h : h \in H\} = \{ 5k + 1 : k \in \Z \} = \{\ldots, -14,-9,-4,1,6,11,\ldots\} = [1]_{5}.\] So the left coset of \(H\) containing \(1\) is the residue class of \(1\) modulo \(5\). Notice that \(H\) is precisely \([0]_{5}\).

This is not a coincidence.

Let \(n\) be a positive integer. Then \(H = \{ nk : k \in \Z \}\) is a subgroup of \((\Z,+)\). For any integer \(a \in \Z\), the left coset \(aH\) of \(H\) containing \(a\) is precisely \[\{a + h \in h \in H\} = \{ a + nk : k \in \Z \} = [a]_{n}.\] Clearly \(H = [0]_{n}\).

Notice that grouping together in sets the integers that are congruent to each other modulo some given \(n\) results in a partitioning of the integers such that each integer appears in exactly one of the residue classes. This partitioning of \(\Z\) is a direct consequence of the fact that congruence modulo \(n\) is what is known as an equivalence relation on a set (more of this in third-year Group Theory).

Example 8.9  

We take \(n = 5\). Then:

\[\begin{eqnarray*} [0]_{5} &=& \{\ldots, -10, -5, 0, 5, 10, \ldots\} \\ {[1]}_{5} &=& \{\ldots, -9, -4, 1, 6, 11, \ldots\} \\ {[2]}_{5} &=& \{\ldots, -8, -3, 2, 7, 12, \ldots\} \\ {[3]}_{5} &=& \{\ldots, -7, -2, 3, 8, 13, \ldots\} \\ {[4]}_{5} &=& \{\ldots, -6, -1, 4, 9, 14, \ldots\}. \end{eqnarray*}\]

Lemma 8.1 Every integer is in exactly one of the residue classes \([0]_n, [1]_n, \ldots ,[n-1]_n\) for a given positive integer \(n\).

Proof.

We note that for \(i\) between \(0\) and \(n-1\) and \(a \in \Z\), \(a \in [i]_{n}\) precisely when \(a\) has remainder \(i\) when divided by \(n\). Therefore every integer belongs to one of the residue classes \([0]_n, [1]_n, \ldots ,[n-1]_n\).

Recall that when we first introduced the concept of \(\Z_n\), we defined this to be the set \(\{0, 1, 2, \ldots , n-1 \}\). Strictly speaking, we should have defined \(\Z_n\) as \[\Z_n = \{[0]_n, [1]_n, [2]_n, \ldots , [n-1]_n \},\] though for practical purposes we tend to use the former representation.

Definition 8.5 Let \(A, B \in \Z_n\) (so \(A\) and \(B\) are residue classes, not numbers). Choose \(a \in A\) and \(b \in B\). Then

  1. \(A+B = [a+b]_n\) or, equivalently, \([a]_n + [b]_n = [a+b]_n\),
  2. \(A \times B = [ab]_n\) or, equivalently, \([a]_n[b]_n = [ab]_n\).

Note that this is a set with two binary operations defined on it. This forms the triple \((\Z_n, +, \times)\) and such a structure is called a ring, provided that certain axioms are satisfied. We shall see more of this in the final chapter.

Example 8.10  

Take \(n = 5\). Then \(\Z_{5} = \{[0]_{5}, [1]_{5}, [2]_{5}, [3]_{5},[4]_{5}\}\). We compute

  1. \([1]_{5} + 2_{5} = [1 + 2]_{5} = [3]_{5}\);
  2. \([3]_{5}[4]_{5} = [3\times 4]_{5} = [2]_{5}\).

It is important to check that these operations are well defined. That is, if we were to choose different members of \(A\) and \(B\), say \(\ap\) and \(\bp\) instead of \(a\) and \(b\) respectively, does \([\ap + \bp]_n = [\ap]_n + [\bp]_n\) and does \([\ap \bp]_n = [\ap]_n[\bp]_n\)? The fact that the answer is in the affirmative follows from statement (g) in the introductory section to this chapter.

Lemma 8.2 \((\zn, +)\) is an abelian group.

Proof.

This is clear from the definition of \(+\) on \(\zn\).

Suppose that we now change the binary operation on \(\zn\) from addition modulo \(n\) to multiplication modulo \(n\). Let us take \(\Z_5\) as an example and construct the operation table (in all of the following examples we shall, for convenience, write \(a\) in place of \([a]_n\)).

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc} \otimes_5&0&1&2&3&4\\ \hline 0&0&1&2&3&4\\ 1&0&1&2&3&4\\ 2&0&2&4&1&3\\ 3&0&3&1&4&2\\ 4&0&4&3&2&1 \end{array}\]

As we can see, \((\Z_5, \times)\) cannot be a group as the element 0 has no inverse; recall that under multiplication the identity element is 1 and in the table above it is clear that there does not exist an element of \(\Z_5\), \(a\) say, such that \(0 \times a = a \times 0 = 1\). So what if we remove 0 from the set and consider \((\Z_5 \backslash \{0\}, \times)\)? It should be clear, from an examination of the table above, that this results in the following group (check the axioms):

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc} \otimes_5&1&2&3&4\\ \hline 1&1&2&3&4\\ 2&2&4&1&3\\ 3&3&1&4&2\\ 4&4&3&2&1 \end{array}\]

The obvious question to ask at this point is whether or not this process works for all \(n \in \N\). Consider \((\Z_8 \backslash \{0\}, \times)\):

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccccc} \otimes_8&1&2&3&4&5&6&7\\ \hline 1&1&2&3&4&5&6&7 \\ 2&2&4&6&0&2&4&6 \\ 3&3&6&1&4&7&2&5 \\ 4&4&0&4&0&4&0&4 \\ 5&5&2&7&4&1&6&3 \\ 6&6&4&2&0&6&4&2 \\ 7&7&6&5&4&3&2&1 \end{array}\]

Clearly this does not form a group for two reasons. First, the set is not closed under \(\otimes_8\) and, second, not all elements have an inverse in the set (1, 3, 5, and 7 have inverses in the set, but 2, 4, and 6 do not). Notice, also, that it is the non-invertible elements that cause the system to be not closed. So, what if we were to remove the elements that have no inverse in the set? \[\begin{array}{c|cccc} \otimes_8&1&3&5&7\\ \hline 1&1&3&5&7\\ 3&3&1&7&5\\ 5&5&7&1&3\\ 7&7&5&3&1 \end{array}\] This system now forms a group. But what is the relationship between the elements of \(\zn\) that have no inverse and \(n\) itself? On the face of the above example, it looks like we simply remove the even numbers from the set. However, if we consider \((\Z_{12}\backslash \{0\}, \times)\), in order for that to be a group we need to remove the elements 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 9, and 10. So how do we identify the invertible elements in \(\zn\)? The answer comes from the following definition:

Definition 8.6 (Reduced Residue System) The reduced residue system modulo \(n\) is denoted and defined by \[\znx = \{ \, [a]_n \in \zn \where \exists \, [b]_n \in \zn \, \text{s.t.} \, [a]_n[b]_n = [1]_n \, \}.\]

Theorem 8.4 For any positive integer \(n\), \(\znx\) is an abelian group.

Proof.

If \(\znx\) is a group then it must be an abelian group since the product is commutative. We therefore verify that \(\znx\) forms a group under multiplication.

Closure:
Let \([a]_{n},[b]_{n} \in \znx\). Then there are elements \([a']_{n},[b']_{n} \in \zn\) such that \([a]_{n}[a']_{n} = [1]_[n] = [b]_{n}[b']_{n}\). Now consider \([ab]_{n}\). Observe \[[ab]_{n}[a'b']_{n} = [(ab)(a'b')]_{n} = [1]_{n}\] since \(ab \equiv 1 \pmod{n}\) and \(a'b' \equiv 1 \pmod{n}\).
Assoociativity:
Let \([a]_n,[b]_n,[c]_{n} \in \znx\). Then, \[\begin{eqnarray*} ([a]_n[b]_{n})[c]_{n} &=& [ab]_{n}[c]_n \\ &=& [(ab)c]_{n} \\ &=& [a(bc)]_{n}\\ &=&[a]_{n}[bc]_{n}\\ &=& [a]_{n}([b]_{n}[c]_n). \end{eqnarray*}\]
Identity:
It is clear that \([1]_n \in \znx\) is the identity element.
Inverses:
Let \([a]_n \in \znx\). By definition, there is an element \([a']_{n} \in \zn\) such that \([a]_{n}[a']_{n} = [1]_n\). Notice that \([a']_{n}[a]_{n} = [1]_{n}\) also. It follows that \([a']_{n} \in \znx\) and every element \([a]_{n} \in \znx\) has an inverse in \(\znx\).

One consequence of the definition is that we can characterise \(\znx\) in a different way, namely as the set of all positive integers less than \(n\) that are relatively prime to \(n\) (from here on in we shall, in general, use a single representative of a residue class to represent the class itself). Note, however, that this is not an alternative definition, just a convenient way of ‘seeing’ \(\znx\). One advantage of this characterisation is that it makes it easy to identify the elements in the set and, hence, to determine the size of the set (more of that later).

Example 8.11 The elements of \(\Z_{15}^{\times}\) are the positive integers that are relatively prime to 15. \[\Z_{15}^{\times} = \{1, 2, 4, 7, 8, 11, 13, 14\}\] and the operation table is \[\begin{array}{c|cccccccc} \otimes_{15}&1&2&4&7&8&11&13&14\\ \hline 1&1&2&4&7&8&11&13&14\\ 2&2&4&8&14&1&7&11&13\\ 4&4&8&1&13&2&14&7&11\\ 7&7&14&13&4&11&2&1&8\\ 8&8&1&2&11&4&13&14&7\\ 11&11&7&14&2&13&1&8&4\\ 13&13&11&7&1&14&8&4&2\\ 14&14&13&11&8&7&4&2&1 \end{array}\]

What we have discussed above leads to the following corollary.

Corollary 8.4 \((\zn \backslash \{[0]_n\}, \times)\) is a group if and only if \(n\) is prime.

Proof.

\(\Rightarrow\): Suppose \((\zn \backslash \{[0]_n\}, \times)\) is a group. Suppose \(n = pq\) for some positive integers \(p,q \le n\). If \(p,q < n\), then \([p]_n,[q]_{n} \in \zn \backslash \{[0]_{n}\}\) and \[[p]_{n}[q]_{n} =[pq]_{n}= [n]_{n} = [0]_{n}.\] This is a contradiction since, by assumption, \((\zn \backslash \{[0]_n\}, \times)\) is a group and so it is closed under time. It follows that one of \(p\) and \(q\) is equal to \(n\) and the other is equal to \(1\). Thus the only divisors of \(n\) and \(1\) and itself — \(n\) is prime.

\(\Leftarrow\): If \(n\) is prime then \(\znx = \zn \backslash \{[0]_{n}\}\) (by the alternative characterisation above). Therefore, \((\zn \backslash \{[0]_n\}, \times)\) is a group since it is precisely \((\znx, \times)\).

8.3 Applications to Number Theory

In the last part of this chapter we shall examine how we can use our knowledge of groups to solve problems in number theory and, in particular, we shall be studying three theorems, namely Fermat’s Little Theorem (FLT), Euler’s Theorem, and Wilson’s Theorem. It is hoped that this very short review of number theoretic problems will not only convince you of the power of the abstract algebra that we have studied to date, but also whet your appetite for the third-year Number Theory and Cryptography module.

We begin with a statement of Fermat’s Little Theorem.

Theorem 8.5 (Fermat’s Little Theorem) Let \(p\) be a prime and let \(a\) be any integer such that \(p\) does not divide \(a\). Then \[a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \modu p).\]

Proof.

First we note that if \(p\) does not divide \(a\), then \([a]_{p} \in \Z_{p}^{\times}\) (\(a\) is co-prime to \(p\)).

The result is now a consequence of Theorem 4.1. We can see this as follows: \(|\Z_{p}^{\times}| = p-1\) since \(\Z_{p}^{\times}= \Z_{p}\backslash\{[0]_{p}\}\). Now Theorem 4.1 states that the order of \([a]_{p}\) divides \(|\Z_{p}^{\times}|\). It follows that \(([a]_{p})^{p-1} = [a^{p-1}]_{p} = [1]_{p}\). Thus \(a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}\).

Example 8.14 What is the principal remainder on dividing \(6^{82}\) by 17?

Solution:

Observe that \(17\) is prime and so we can make use of Fermat’s Little Theorem to simplify this calculation. Write \(82 = (5 \times 16) + 2\). Then \(6^{82} = (6^{16})^{5} \times 6^2\). By FLT, \(6^{16} \equiv 1 \pmod{17}\). Therefore, \[(6^{16})^{5} \times 6^2 \equiv 6^2 \pmod{17} \equiv 2 \pmod 17.\]

Example 8.15 Show that, for every positive integer \(n\), the number \(n^{33}-n\) is divisible by 15.

First observe that \(n^{33}-n = n(n^{32}-1)\). Next observe that \(15| n^33 - n\) if and only if both \(5\) and \(3\) divide \(n^{33} -n\). Since 5 and 3 are prime we can make use of FLT. We show that both \(5\) and \(3\) divide \(n(n^{32}-1)\).

  • \(3|(n^{33}-n)\): If \(3\) divides \(n\) we are done. If \(3\) does not divide \(n\), then \(n\) and \(3\) are relatively prime. Write \(32 = 2\times 16\). Then \[n^{32} \equiv (n^2)^{16} \equiv 1 \pmod{3}\] by FLT. Therefore \(n^{32} -1 \equiv 0 \pmod{3}\) and \(3|(n^32 -1)\).
  • \(5|(n^{33}-n)\): If \(5\) divides \(n\) we are done. If \(5\) does not divide \(n\), then \(n\) and \(5\) are relatively prime. Write \(32 = 4\times 8\). Then \[n^{32} \equiv (n^4)^{8} \equiv 1 \pmod{5}\] by FLT. Therefore \(n^{32} -1 \equiv 0 \pmod{5}\) and \(5|(n^32 -1)\).

The obvious question to ask is whether we can generalise the above result to include composite moduli. Consider the conditions that apply for FLT to work; first, we work modulo a prime, second we have that \(p\) does not divide \(a\). Recall that if \(p\) is prime the statement \(p\) does not divide \(a\) is equivalent to saying that \(p\) and \(a\) are relatively prime. So, a naive approach to extending the theorem to composite numbers would be to conjecture as follows:

Let \(a\) and \(n\) be integers such that \(\greatcd (a, n)=1\). Then \(a^{n-1} \equiv 1 \modu n)\).

Does this work?

This does not work. A counterexample is as follows: take \(n = 4\) and \(a=3\). Then \(\gcd(a,n) = \gcd(3,4) =1\). However \[3^{3} \equiv 3 \pmod{4}.\]

So, is there a way in which we can generalise FLT to cover non-prime moduli? The answer is ‘yes’ and the solution lies in considering the exponent of \(a\); if we are raising \(a\) to the power of \(p-1\), where \(p\) is prime, that is the same as raising it to the power of the order of \(\Z_p^{\times}\) since this group has \(p-1\) elements (a fact that was used in our proof of FLT). We consider, then, raising \(a\) to the power of the order of \(\znx\) where \(n \in \N\) and working modulo \(n\).

Definition 8.7 (Euler Totient Function) Let \(m, n \in \N\). The Euler totient function, denoted \(\phi\), is the function \(\phi : \N \rightarrow \N\) defined by \[\phi (n) = | \, \{m \in \N \where m \leq n \, \text{and} \, \greatcd (m, n)=1 \} \, |, \;\; \forall \, n \in \N.\]

Note that this is, in fact, \(| \znx |\).

Example 8.16  

\(\phi(30) = |\{1,7,11,13,17,19,23,29\}| = 8\).

We can now generalise FLT to include composite moduli as follows:

Theorem 8.6 (Euler’s Theorem) Let \(n \in \N\) and \(a \in \Z\) such that \(a\) and \(n\) are relatively prime. Then, \[a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \modu n).\]

Proof.

The proof is almost identical to the proof of FLT. The key ingredient is once more Lagrange’s Theorem.

Let \(a \in \Z\) such that \(\gcd(a,n) =1\). Then \([a]_{n} \ne [0]_{n}\) and \([a]_{n} \in \znx\). Now as \(|\znx| = \phi(n)|\), we have, by Lagrange’s Theorem that \[([a]_{n})^{\phi(n)} = [1]_{n}.\] Therefore \(a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}\) as required.

Hopefully, the success or failure of our chosen attempts to generalise FLT will now make complete sense.

Let us revisit the \(n=4\) and \(a =3\) example. Notice that \(\phi(4) = 2\) and \(3^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{4}\).

This function, together with Euler’s Theorem, prove to be extremely useful tools in solving number-theoretic problems.

Example 8.17 Find the unit digit of \(3^{101}\).

Notice that if \(d \in \{0,1,\ldots, 9\}\) is the unit digit of \(3^{101}\), then \(3^{101} \equiv d \pmod{10}\) since there is a \(q \in \Z\) such that \(3^{101} = 10q + d\). We use Euler’s Theorem to compute \(d\). Note that \(\phi(10) =|\{1,3,7,9\}| = 4\) and \(101 = 4 \times 25 + 1\). It follows that \[3^{101} = (3^{4})^{25} \times 3) \equiv 1 \times 3 \pmod{10}.\] The last digit of \(3^{101}\) is therefore \(3\).

Notice the very close link between FLT and Euler’s Theorem.

FLT is a corollary of Euler’s Theorem: if \(p\) is prime, \(\phi(p) = p-1\) and so for \(a \in \Z\) relatively prime to \(p\), \(a^{\phi(p)} = a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}\).

Theorem 8.7 (Wilson’s Theorem) If \(p\) is prime, then \((p-1)! \equiv -1 \modu p)\).

Proof.

We begin with the following observation. Let \(1 \le x \le p-1\) and suppose that \(x\) is its own inverse in \(\Z_{p}^{\times}\). This means that \(x^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{p}\). Rearranging, \(x^2 -1 \equiv 0 \pmod{p}\). Therefore \(p|(x^2-1)\). Using the difference of two squares \(p|((x-1)(x+1)\). Since \(p\) is prime either \(p|(x-1)\) or \(p|(x+1)\). Thus \(x \equiv \pm 1 \pmod{p}\). Since \(1 \le x \le p-1\), then \(x = 1\) or \(x =p-1\). Thus \(x \in \Z_{p}^{\times}\backslash\{1,p-1\}\), the inverse of \(x \in \Z_{p}^{\times}\) is not equal to \(x\). For \(2 \le x < p-1\) write \(x^{-1}\) for the element of \(y \in \{2,3,\ldots, p-2\}\) such that \(xy \equiv 1 \pmod{p}\). Thus we can find elements \(x_1, x_2 ,\ldots, x_{\left(\frac{p-3}{2}\right)} \in \{2,3,\ldots, p-2\}\) such that

\[(p-1)! = 1\times (x_1 \times x_1^{-1})\times (x_2\times x_2^{-1}) \ldots \left(x_{\left(\frac{p-3}{2}\right)} x_{\left(\frac{p-3}{2}\right)}^{-1}\right) \times (p-1).\]

Working modulo \(p\), we have \[\begin{eqnarray*} (p-1)! &=& 1\times (2 \times 2^{-1})\times (3\times 3^{-1}) \ldots \left(\left(\frac{p-1}{2}\right) \left(\frac{p-1}{2}\right)^{-1}\right) \times (p-1) \\ &\equiv& 1\times 1 \times 1 \ldots 1 \times (p-1) \pmod{p} \\ &\equiv& (p-1) \pmod{p} \\ &\equiv& -1 \pmod{p} \end{eqnarray*}\]

as required.

Example 8.19 Find the principal remainder when 15! is divided by 17.

We use Wilson’s Theorem: \(16! = 16 \times 15! \equiv -1 \pmod{17}\). It follows that \(15! \equiv (-1) (16)^{-1}\pmod{17}\). Since \(16\) is its own inverse in \(\Z_{17}^{\times}\). It follows that \(15! \equiv -16 \pmod{17}\); \(15! \equiv 1 \pmod{17}\). The principal remainder when \(15!\) is divided by 17 is therefore 1.

Example 8.20 Find the inverses of 10, 9 and 8 in \(\Z_{11}^{\times}\) and hence find the remainder when 7! is divided by 11.

We note that \(10 \equiv -1 \pmod{11}\) and so \(10\) is its own inverse i \(\Z_{11}\).

Now \(9 \equiv -2 \pmod{11}\). Therefore \[9 \times 5 \equiv -10\pmod{11} \equiv 1 \pmod{11}\] and so \(9^{-1} = 5\)

Lastly \(8 \equiv -3 \pmod{11}\) and so \[8 \times -4 \equiv 12 \pmod{11} \equiv 1 \pmod{11}.\] It follows that \(8^{-1} = 7\).

We apply Wilson’s Theorem to compute the remainder of \(7!\) when divided by \(11\). We have: \[10! = 10 \times 9 \times 8 \times 7! \equiv -1 \pmod{11}.\] Therefore \[7! \equiv -(10^{-1}\times 9^{-1}\times 8^{-1})\pmod{11}.\] Hence \[7! \equiv 35 \pmod{11}\equiv 2 \pmod{11}.\]

Example 8.21 Use Wilson’s Theorem to find the principal remainder on dividing 51! by 61.

Applying Wilson’s Theorem: \[51! \equiv -(60 \cdot 59 \cdot \ldots \cdot 52)^{-1}\pmod{61}.\] Now we use the fact that \[60 \equiv -1 \pmod{61}, 59 \equiv -2\pmod{61}, \ldots, 52 \equiv -9\pmod{61}.\] Therefore \[60 \cdot 59 \cdot \ldots \cdot 52 \equiv -(9!) \pmod{61}.\]

Now \(9! = 7! \times 72\). We decompose \(7! = 7 \times 60 \times 12\). Thus \[7! \equiv - 84 \pmod{61} \equiv 38 \pmod {61}.\] On the other hand \(72 \equiv 11 \pmod {61}\). Therefore \(9! \equiv 38 \times 11 \pmod{61} \equiv 52 \pmod{61}\).

Now we compute \(52^{-1} \in \Z_{61}^{\times}\). We use the fact that \(52 \equiv -9 = -3 \times 3 \pmod{61}\). Now \(3^{-1} \in \Z_{61}^{\times}\) is \(-20\). Therefore \[(-9)^{-1} = (-3)^{-1}\times 3^{-1} \equiv 20 \times -20 = 27 \pmod{61}.\]

It follows that \(51! \equiv 9! \equiv 27 \pmod{61}\).

It is worth noting that the converse of Wilson’s Theorem is also true (it is, in fact, an if and only if statement), that is if \((p-1)! \equiv -1 \modu p)\) then \(p\) is prime. This is, however, not much use as a primality test!

  1. In what follows we repeat that procedure at each step. \[\begin{align} 440 &= 2 \times 189 + 62 \tag{1}\\ 189 &= 3 \times 62 + 3 \tag{2}\\ 62 &= 20 \times 3 + 2 \tag{3}\\ 3 &= 1 \times 2 + 1 \tag{4}\\ 2 &= 2 \times 1 \tag{5} \end{align}\] So, gcd \((440, 189)=1\).

    We can now work backwards from the gcd as follows: \[\begin{eqnarray*} 1 &=& 3 - (1 \times 2)\\ &=& 3 - (62 - (20 \times 3)) \hspace{5cm} \text{using} \quad (3)\\ &=& (21 \times 3) - (1 \times 62)\\ &=& (21 \times (189 - (3 \times 62))) - (1 \times 62) \hspace{2.5cm} \text{using} \quad (2)\\ &=& (21 \times 189) - (64 \times 62)\\ &=& (21 \times 189) - (64 \times (440 - (2 \times 189))) \hspace{2cm} \text{using} \quad (1)\\ &=& (-64 \times 440) + (149 \times 189). \end{eqnarray*}\]

    So \(s = 149\) and \(t = -64\).

  2. We compute \(\gcd(1343,391)\):

    \[\begin{align} 1343 &= 3 \times 391 + 170 \tag{6}\\ 391 &= 2 \times 170 + 51 \tag{7}\\ 170 &= 3 \times 51 + 17 \tag{8}\\ 51 &= 3 \times 17 \tag{9} \end{align}\]

    So, gcd \((1343, 391)=17\).

    We can now work backwards from the gcd as follows: \[\begin{eqnarray*} 17 &=& 170 - (3 \times 51)\\ &=& 170 - (3 \times (391 - (2 \times 170))) \hspace{3.1cm} \text{using} \quad (7)\\ &=& (7 \times 170) - (3 \times 391)\\ &=& (7 \times (1343 - (3 \times 391))) - (3 \times 391) \hspace{2cm} \text{using} \quad (6)\\ &=& (7 \times 1343) - (24 \times 391). \end{eqnarray*}\]

    So \(s = 7\) and \(t = -24\).

  3. We compute \(\gcd(-419,52)\):

    So, gcd \((975, 121)=1\).

Question 8.3

Show that \(\Z^{\times}_{12}\) is isomorphic to \(\Z_2 \times \Z_2\) and write down an isomorphism between the two groups.

Solution 8.3

\(\Z^{\times}_{12} = \{1, 5, 7, 11\}\), a group of order 4.

We know that there are only two isomorphically distinct groups of order 4, namely \(\Z_4\) and \(\Z_2 \times \Z_2\). We also know that \(\Z_4\) is cyclic , whereas \(\Z_2 \times \Z_2\) is not. So, in order to show that \(\Z^{\times}_{12}\) is isomorphic to \(\Z_2 \times \Z_2\), we simply need to demonstrate that it is not cyclic. The easiest way to do this is to show that \(\Z^{\times}_{12}\) does not contain any element of period 4 (if the group is cyclic it must have a generator which must have a period equal to the order of the group). Note that

\[\begin{eqnarray*} 5^2 &=& 25 \equiv 1 \modu 12)\\ 7^2 &=& 49 \equiv 1 \modu 12)\\ 11^2 &=& 121 \equiv 1 \modu 12). \end{eqnarray*}\]

Hence every element has period 2, except for the identity which has period 1. So \(\Z^{\times}_{12}\) is isomorphic to \(\Z_2 \times \Z_2\) and not \(\Z_4\).

As every element of each of those two groups (identity excepted) has period 2, an isomorphism is not difficult to find. For example \[\theta = \left(\begin{array}{cccc}(0, 0)&(0,1 )&(1, 0)&(1, 1)\\1&5&7&11\end{array} \right).\]

Question 8.4

Use Fermat’s Little theorem to find the principal remainder on dividing:

  1. \(5^{25}\) by 13;
  2. \(17^{44}\) by 7;
  3. \(19^{53}\) by 11.
Solution 8.4

Recall Fermat’s Little Theorem (FLT):

If \(p\) is prime and \(a\) is an integer such that \(p \nmid a\), then \(a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \modu p)\).

  1. In the notation of FLT let \(p=13\) and \(a = 5\). Now, \(13 \nmid 5\), so by FLT we have that \(5^{12} \equiv 1 \modu 13)\). Then, \[\begin{eqnarray*} 5^{25} &=& 5 \times 5^{24}\\ &=& 5 \times (5^{12})^2\\ &\equiv& 5 \times 1^2 \modu 13) \hspace{3cm} \mbox{since} \, 5^{12} \equiv 1 \, \modu 13)\\ &\equiv& 5 \modu 13) \end{eqnarray*}\] So, the principal remainder on dividing \(5^{25}\) by \(13\) is \(5\).

  2. In the notation of FLT let \(p=7\) and \(a = 17\). Now, \(7 \nmid 17\), so by FLT we have that \(17^{6} \equiv 1 \modu 7)\). Then, \[\begin{eqnarray*} 17^{44} &=& 17^2 \times 17^{42}\\ &=& 17^2 \times (17^{6})^7\\ &\equiv& 17^2 \times 1^7 \modu 7) \hspace{3cm} \mbox{since} \, 17^{6} \equiv 1 \, \modu 7)\\ &\equiv& 3^2 \modu 7) \hspace{4cm} \mbox{since} \, 17 \equiv 3 \, \modu 7)\\ &\equiv& 9 \modu 7)\\ &\equiv& 2 \modu 7) \end{eqnarray*}\] So, the principal remainder on dividing \(17^{44}\) by \(7\) is \(2\).

  3. In the notation of FLT let \(p=11\) and \(a = 19\). Now, \(11 \nmid 19\), so by FLT we have that \(19^{10} \equiv 1 \modu 11)\). Then, \[\begin{eqnarray*} 19^{53} &=& 19^3 \times 19^{50}\\ &=& 19^3 \times (19^{10})^5\\ &\equiv& 19^3 \times 1^5 \modu 11) \hspace{3cm} \mbox{since} \, 19^{10} \equiv 1 \, \modu 11)\\ &\equiv& 8^3 \modu 11) \hspace{4cm} \mbox{since} \, 19 \equiv 8 \, \modu 11)\\ &\equiv& (-3)^3 \modu 11) \hspace{3.5cm} \mbox{since} \, 8 \equiv -3 \, \modu 11)\\ &\equiv& -27 \modu 11)\\ &\equiv& 6 \modu 11) \end{eqnarray*}\] So, the principal remainder on dividing \(19^{53}\) by \(11\) is \(6\).

Question 8.5
Show that \(2^{11, 213} - 1\) is not divisible by 11.
Solution 8.5

We can use Fermat’ Little Theorem to solve this. Recall what the theorem says:

If \(p\) is prime, \(a \in \Z\) and \(p\) does not divide \(a\), then \(a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \modu p)\).

Let \(p = 11\) and \(a = 2\). Now 11 does not divide 2, so we can use FLT and \[2^{11-1} = 2^{10} \equiv 1 \modu 11).\] Now \[\begin{eqnarray*} 2^{11,213} - 1 &=& 2^3 (2^{11,210}) - 1\\ &=& 8 (2^{10})^{1121} - 1\\ &\equiv&8 (1)^{1121} - 1 \; \modu 11)\\ &\equiv&8 - 1 \; \modu 11)\\ &\equiv& 7 \modu 11). \end{eqnarray*}\]

So, \(2^{11,213} - 1\) leaves principal remainder 7 on division by 11.

Note that 11,213 is prime and it can be shown that \(2^{11,213} - 1\) is prime. Primes of the form \(2^p - 1\) are called Mersenne primes. The largest known prime is a Mersenne prime; discovered in 2017 it is \(2^{77,232,917} - 1\) and has 23,249,425 digits.

Question 8.6
Find the inverses of 16, 17 and 18 in \(\Z^{\times}_{19}\). Use those results, together with Wilson’s Theorem, to find the remainder when 15! is divided by 19.
Solution 8.6

\(\Z^{\times}_{19} = \{1, 2, 3, \ldots , 16, 17, 18\}\). We know from the proof of Wilson’s Theorem that in \(\Z^{\times}_p\), where \(p\) is prime, the self-inverse elements are 1 and \(p-1\). So, the inverse of 18 in \(\Z^{\times}_{19}\) is 18 (which, of course, is equivalent to \(-1 \modu 19)\)).

Now, \(17^{-1} = 9\) since \(17 \times 9 = 153\) and \(153 \equiv 1 \modu 19)\).

Similarly, \(16^{-1} = 6\).

Recall that Wilson’s Theorem states that if \(p\) is prime then \((p-1)! \equiv -1 \modu p)\). So, with \(p=19\) we have \(18! \equiv -1 \modu 19)\).\\ Now, using the above results, \[\begin{eqnarray*} 15! &=& 18! \times 18^{-1} \times 17^{-1} \times 16^{-1}\\ &\equiv& (-1) \times (-1) \times 9 \times 6 \modu 19)\\ &\equiv& 54 \modu 19)\\ &\equiv& 16 \modu 19) \end{eqnarray*}\] Hence, the principal remainder is 16.

Question 8.7

Let \(p\) be a prime except 2 and 5. Deduce from Fermat’s Little Theorem that there is a multiple of \(p\), all of whose digits in decimal notation are 9’s.

[Example: \(142857\times 7=999999\).]
Solution 8.7

Note that \(999 \ldots 9 = 10^k - 1\) for some \(k \in \Z\). So, \[\begin{eqnarray*} np = 999 \ldots 9 &\Leftrightarrow& 10^k - 1 = np\\ &\Leftrightarrow& 10^k \equiv 1 \,\modu p) \end{eqnarray*}\] Now, \(p \neq 2\) and \(p \neq 5\), hence \(10\) is not a multiple of \(p\), that is \(p \, \nmid \,10\). Hence, by FLT, \(10^{p-1} \equiv 1 \,\modu p)\) with \(p \neq 2, \, 5\). Then, with \(p=7\), say, \(10^6 \equiv 1 \, \modu 7)\) and so \(999,999 = 7n\) for some \(n\).

Question 8.8
Consider the set of Hilbert Numbers, \(\Sbb = \{ n \in \N \, | \, n \equiv 1 \modu 4) \}\). This is the set of all integers that are of the form \(4k + 1\), where \(k \in \N\). Find the first five ‘primes’ in this set (called Hilbert Primes). What is the smallest number in \(\Sbb\) that does not have a unique factorisation in Hilbert Primes?
Solution 8.8

First, it helps to visualise what numbers are actually in the set. Since we are going to be looking at primes in the set we need only consider members of \(\Sbb\) that are positive. So \[\Sbb = \{1, 5, 9, 13, 17, 21, 25, 29, 33, 37, \ldots \}.\]

We then need a suitable definition of ‘prime’ within the context of this set. If we use our usual definition for primes in \(\N\), we need to be careful that we refer to divisibility within the set. So, we define a Hilbert prime as a member of \(\Sbb\) greater than 1 that is divisible only by 1 (which is in \(\Sbb\)) and itself. Then, the first five Hilbert primes are 5, 9, 13, 17 and 21. Note that 9 and 21 are both divisible by 3 in \(\Z\), but 3 is not in \(\Sbb\).

Like the case of \(\E\) in the lectures, the unique factorisation principle in \(\N\) breaks down in \(\Sbb\). The first number in \(\Sbb\) to exhibit multiple prime factorisations is \[441 = 9 \times 49 = 21 \times 21.\]
