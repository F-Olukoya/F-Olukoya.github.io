4  Holomorphic functions

The following definition is a key concept for this course.

Definition 4.1 A complex function \(f\colon U\to\C\) with domain an open set \(U\subset \C\) is complex differentiable at a point \(z_0\in U\) if the limit

\[f'(z_0)=\frac{\6f}{\6z}(z_0)=\lim_{z\to z_0} \frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0} \tag{4.1}\]

exists. We call \(f\) holomorphic on \(U\) if \(f\) is complex differentiable at every \(z_0\in U.\) The set of all holomorphic functions on \(U\) is denoted \(\cO(U).\) A function that is holomorphic1 on \(U=\C\) is entire.

This definition is structurally the same as for real functions. For example, \(f\) is continuous at \(z_0\) since multiplying Equation 4.1 by \(0=\lim_{z\to z_0} (z-z_0)\) gives \[0 = \lim_{z\to z_0} f(z)-f(z_0)\implies f(z_0)=\lim_{z\to z_0} f(z).\]

Other formal proofs carry over as well and show the following.

Proposition 4.1 Let \(f, g\colon U\to\C\) be complex differentiable at \(z_0.\) Then:

  1. \(f+g\) is complex differentiable at \(z_0\) with \[(f+g)'(z_0)=f'(z_0)+g'(z_0).\]
  2. \(fg\) is complex differentiable at \(z_0\) with\[(fg)'(z_0)=f'(z_0)g(z_0)+f(z_0)g'(z_0).\]
  3. If \(g(z_0)\neq0,\) then \(f/g\) is complex differentiable at \(z_0\) with\[(f/g)'(z_0)=\frac{f'(z_0)g(z_0)-f(z_0)g'(z_0)}{g(z_0)^2}.\]

Proof.

Exercise, using the algebra of limits.

Example 4.1  

Directly from Equation 4.1 we find that the constant function \(f(z)=c\) is entire with \(f'(z)=0.\) The identity function \(f(z)=z\) is entire with \(f'(z)=1.\) Proposition 4.1 and induction then imply that all polynomials \[f(z)=a_nz^n+a_{n-1}z^{n-1}+\ldots+a_1z+a_0\]

are entire with \[f'(z)=na_nz^{n-1}+(n-1)a_{n-1}z^{n-2}+\ldots+a_1.\]

It then follows from Proposition 4.1 part c. that rational functions are holomorphic on their domain of definition.

Proposition 4.2 Let \(f\colon U\to\C,\) \(g\colon V\to\C\) be complex functions with \(f(U)\subset V,\) and \(U,\) \(V\) open. Suppose that \(f\) is complex differentiable at \(z_0\in U\) and that \(g\) is complex differentiable at \(w_0=f(z_0)\in V.\)

Then \(g\circ f\) is complex differentiable at \(z_0\) and\[(g\circ f)'(z_0)=g'(w_0)f'(z_0). \tag{4.2}\]

Proof.

We can equivalently rewrite Equation 4.1 as \[f(z_0+h)=f(z_0)+hf'(z_0)+h\rho(h),\qquad\lim_{h\to0}\rho(h)=0, \tag{4.3}\] where the function \(\rho(h),\) \(h\neq0,\) is defined by this equation. Similarly, \[g(w_0+k)=g(w_0)+kg'(w_0)+k\si(k), \qquad\lim_{k\to0}\si(k)=0. \tag{4.4}\] Then \[\begin{align*} &\frac{(g\circ f)(z_0+h) - (g\circ f)(z_0)}{h}= \frac{g\bigl(w_0+\overset{\smash{k=}}{\overbrace{hf'(z_0)+h\rho(h)}}\;\bigr)-g(w_0)}{h}\\ &=f'(z_0)g'(w_0)+\bigl[\rho(h)g'(w_0)+(f'(z_0)+\rho(h))\,\si(hf'(z_0)+h\rho(h))\bigr], \end{align*}\] where the first equality follows from Equation 4.3, the second from Equation 4.4 and the square bracket tends to zero as \(h\to0,\) using the chain rule for limits to see that \(\lim_{h\to0}\si(hf'(z_0)+h\rho(h))=\lim_{k\to 0}\si(k)=0.\)

One should view Equation 4.3 as a short Taylor series expansion: near the point \(z_0\) the function \(f\) is given to zeroth order by a constant \(f(z_0),\) with first order correction given by a complex linear function \(h\mapsto hf'(z_0),\) plus a higher order term \(h\rho(h)\) which vanishes infinitesimally (after dividing by \(h\)).

We next discuss how complex differentiability compares to real differentiability. Regard a complex function as a real multivariable map \[f=(u,v)\colon U\longra\R^2,\qquad\text{$U\subset\R^2$ open set.}\]

Recall that \(f\) is real differentiable at \(z_0=(x_0,y_0)\) if there exists a matrix \(J_f(z_0)\in \M_{2\t 2}(\R),\) called the Jacobian, so that Equation 4.3 holds with the complex multiplication \(hf'(z_0)\) replaced by the matrix vector multiplication \(J_f(z_0)h.\) Real differentiability at \(z_0\) implies that the partial derivatives at \(z_0\) exist and

\[J_f(z_0)= \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\6u}{\6x}(z_0) & \frac{\6u}{\6y}(z_0)\\ \frac{\6v}{\6x}(z_0) & \frac{\6v}{\6y}(z_0) \end{pmatrix}. \tag{4.5}\]

Recall that the converse requires the additional assumptions that the partial derivatives exist in a neighborhood of \(z_0\) and are continuous at \(z_0.\)

Proposition 4.3 For a complex function \(f=u+iv\) the following are equivalent:

  1. \(f\) is complex differentiable at \(z_0\)
  2. \(f\) is real differentiable at \(z_0\) and the Cauchy–Riemann equations \[\frac{\6u}{\6x}(z_0) = \frac{\6v}{\6y}(z_0), \qquad \frac{\6u}{\6y}(z_0) = -\frac{\6v}{\6x}(z_0), \tag{4.6}\] hold.

In this case, \(f'(z_0)=\frac{\6u}{\6x}+i\frac{\6v}{\6x}.\)

Proof.

The two definitions are identical except that the complex multiplication \(f'(z_0)h\) by \(h\in\C\) is replaced by the matrix vector multiplication \(J_f(z_0)h\) by \(h\in\R^2.\) Our task is to compare these two notions. For this we simply observe that by Proposition 1.2 \(J_f(z_0)\) is \(\C\)-linear if and only if Equation 4.6 holds.

Example 4.2  

For the exponential function, \(u=e^x\cos(y),\) \(v=e^x\sin(y).\) Then the partial derivatives exist \[\begin{align*} \frac{\6u}{\6x}&=e^x\cos(y), &\frac{\6u}{\6y}&=-e^x\sin(y), &\frac{\6v}{\6x}&=e^x\sin(y), &\frac{\6v}{\6y}&=e^x\cos(y), \end{align*}\] are continuous functions on \(\R^2,\) and satisfy the Cauchy–Riemann equations. It follows that \(\exp\) is entire with

\[\exp'(z)=\frac{\6u}{\6x}+i\frac{\6v}{\6x}=\exp(z). \tag{4.7}\]

Example 4.3  

The hyperbolic cosine \(\cosh(z)=\frac{e^z+e^{-z}}{2}\) and the hyperbolic sine function \(\sinh(z)=\frac{e^z-e^{-z}}{2}\) are entire with \[\sinh'(z)=\cosh(z),\qquad\cosh'(z)=\sinh(z).\]

Since \(\cosh(z)=0\iff e^{2z}=-1\iff z\in\frac{\pi i}{2}+\pi i\Z\) the hyperbolic tangent \(\tanh(z)=\frac{\sinh(z)}{\cosh(z)}\) is a holomorphic function \[\tanh\colon\C\setminus\left(\frac{\pi i}{2}+\pi i\Z\right)\longra\C,\]

with

\[\tanh'(z)=\frac{\cosh(z)^2-\sinh(z)^2}{\cosh(z)^2}=1-\tanh(z)^2.\]Similarly, the hyperbolic cotangent \(\coth(z)=\frac{\cosh(z)}{\sinh(z)}\) is a holomorphic function \(\coth\colon\C\setminus\pi i\Z\to\C\) with\[\coth'(z)=1-\coth(z)^2.\]

Example 4.4  

The trigonometric sine \(\sin(z)=-i\sinh(iz)\) and the cosine \(\cos(z)=\cosh(iz)\) are entire with \(\sin'(z)=\cos(z),\) \(\cos'(z)=-\sin(z).\) The tangent \(\tan(z)=\frac{\sin(z)}{\cos(z)}\) is a holomorphic function on \(\C\setminus\left(\frac{\pi}{2}+\pi\Z\right)\) with \(\tan'(z)=1+\tan(z)^2.\) The cotangent \(\cot(z)=\frac{\cos(z)}{\sin(z)}\) is a holomorphic function on \(\C\setminus\pi\Z\) with \(\cot'(z)=1-\cot(z)^2.\)

Example 4.5  

\(f(z)=\bar{z}=x-iy\) has all partial derivatives of all orders, but is not complex differentiable at any \(z_0\in\C\) since \(\frac{\6u}{\6x}=1 \neq \frac{\6v}{\6y}=-1.\)

Theorem 4.1 Let \(f\colon U\to \C\) be a holomorphic function and \(z_0\in U\) with \(f'(z_0)\neq0.\) Then there exist open sets \(V, W\subset\C\) with \(z_0\in V\subset U\) and \(w_0=f(z_0)\in W\) with the property that the restriction \(f|_V\) becomes a bijection \(V\to W\) with holomorphic inverse function \((f|_V)^{-1}\colon W\to V\) and \[\frac{\6(f|_V)^{-1}}{\6w}(w)=\frac{1}{f'\bigl((f|_V)^{-1}(w)\bigr)}. \tag{4.8}\]

Proof.

As in the proof of Proposition 4.3, we view \(f\) as a differentiable real multivariable map \(U\to\R^2.\) Since \(f'(z_0)=\frac{\6u}{\6x}(z_0)+i\frac{\6u}{\6y}(z_0)\neq0\) we have \[\begin{align*} \det J_f(z_0)&= \frac{\6u}{\6x}(z_0)\frac{\6v}{\6y}(z_0)-\frac{\6v}{\6x}(z_0)\frac{\6u}{\6y}(z_0)\\ &= \left( \frac{\6u}{\6x}(z_0) \right)^2+\left( \frac{\6u}{\6y}(z_0) \right)^2\neq0. \end{align*}\] where the first equality follows from Equation 4.5 and the second from Equation 4.6. We may thus apply the inverse function theorem from multivariable calculus. This gives open sets with \(z_0\in V\subset U, w_0\in W\) such that \(f|_V\colon V\to W\) is a bijection and \((f|_V)^{-1}\colon W\to V\) is real differentiable. In particular, \(\det J_f(z)\neq0\) for all \(z\in V\) (chain rule). Let \(w\in W\) and \(z=f^{-1}(w).\) Then \[J_{(f|_V)^{-1}}(w)=J_f(z)^{-1}=\frac{1}{\det J_f(z)} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\6v}{\6y}(z) & -\frac{\6u}{\6y}(z)\\ -\frac{\6v}{\6x}(z) & \frac{\6u}{\6x}(z) \end{pmatrix},\]

so the Cauchy–Riemann equations are satisfied at every \(w\in W\) for the inverse function, so \((f|_V)^{-1}\) is holomorphic. Finally, Equation 4.8 follows by applying the chain rule Equation 4.2 to \(f|_V\circ (f|_V)^{-1}=\id_W\) which gives \[(f|_V)'\bigl((f|_V)^{-1}(w)\bigr)\cdot\frac{\6(f|_V)^{-1}}{\6w}(w)=1.\]

Remark 4.1. Our proof is a simple application of the ordinary inverse function theorem. We will see two different proofs later in the course.

Example 4.6  

Recall from Proposition 2.1 that \(\exp|_S\colon S\to\C^-\) is a bijection with the logarithm as inverse function. As \(\exp'(z)=\exp(z)\neq0,\) we conclude from Theorem 4.1 that \(\log\colon\C^-\to S\) is holomorphic with\[\log'(z)=1/z. \tag{4.9}\]

  • \(x\mapsto x^3\) is a real differentiable bijection whose inverse function is not differentiable at \(x=0.\) What about the complex analogue \(z\mapsto z^{1/3}\)?

4.1 Exercises

Exercise 4.1

Write the following complex functions in the form \(f=u+iv\): \[f_1(z)=\sin(z),\qquad f_2(z)=e^{z^2},\qquad f_3(z)=\cosh(z)\]

Exercise 4.2

At which points \(z\in\C\) are the following functions complex differentiable? At which points are they real differentiable? \[\begin{align*} f_1(z)&=z,&f_2(z)&=\ol{z}, &f_3(z)&=z^3+z,\\ f_4(z)&=\frac{1}{2iz}, &f_5(z)&=|z|^2, &f_6(z)&=\frac{|z|^2}{\ol{z}} \end{align*}\]

Exercise 4.3

Let \(f\colon V\to W\) be a bijection of open sets \(V, W\subset\C.\) Assume that \(f\) is complex differentiable at \(z_0\) and that \(f^{-1}\) is complex differentiable at \(w_0=f(z_0).\) Show that \(f'(z_0)\neq0.\) (The same result holds for real differentiable maps to show \(\det J_f(z_0)\neq0\).)

Exercise 4.4

Let \(f\colon U\to\C\) be a holomorphic function with domain an open set \(U\subset\C.\) Suppose also that \(f'(z)\) is holomorphic. Write \(f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y),\) where \(z=x+iy.\) As we will see, a consequence of \(f\) being holomorphic, is that \(u\) and \(v\) have continuous second order derivatives. Show that:

  1. \(|f'(z)|^2=\Bigl(\frac{\6u}{\6x}\Bigr)^2+\Bigl(\frac{\6v}{\6x}\Bigr)^2=\Bigl(\frac{\6u}{\6y}\Bigr)^2+\Bigl(\frac{\6v}{\6y}\Bigr)^2\)
  2. Both \(u,\) \(v\) satisfy the Laplace equation \(\Delta(u)=\Delta(v)=0,\) where the Laplace operator is defined as \(\De=\frac{\6^2}{\6 x^2}+\frac{\6^2}{\6 y^2}.\)
  3. Fix \(n\in\N.\) Use b. to find a solution to \(\De(u)=0\) that satisfies \(u(z)=\cos(n\th)\) for all \(|z|=1,\) \(z=e^{i\th}\) on the unit circle.
  4. Does the converse to b. hold?
Exercise 4.5

Find subdomains where \(\sin,\cos,\sinh,\cosh,\tan,\tanh\) are injective. Compute the derivative of an inverse using the chain rule. Deduce an explicit formula for the derivative of an inverse.

Exercise 4.6

Show that the set \(\cO(U)\) of holomorphic functions on \(U\) becomes a ring under the operations of point-wise addition and multiplication: \[\begin{align*} (f+g)(z)&=f(z)+g(z), &(fg)(z)&=f(z)g(z). \end{align*}\]

Exercise 4.7

Let \(f\colon\C\to\C\) be an entire function with image \(f(\C)\subset\R.\) Prove that \(f\) is constant.

Exercise 4.8

Let \(U\) be an open set and assume that \(U\) is path-connected, meaning that for all \(z_0, z_1\in U\) there exists a continuously differentiable map \(\ga\colon[0,1]\to U\) such that \(\ga(0)=z_0,\) \(\ga(1)=z_1.\)

  1. Let \(f\colon U\to\C\) be a holomorphic function such that \(f'(z)=0\) for all \(z\in U.\) Prove that \(f(z)\) is a constant function.

    Hint: Consider the real and imaginary parts of \(f\circ\ga.\)

  2. Show that the only entire functions \(f(z)\) satisfying \(f'(z)=C f(z)\) for a constant \(C\in\C\) are the functions \(f(z)=De^{Cz},\) \(D\in\C.\)

  3. Find all entire solutions \(f(z)\) of \(f''(z)=f(z).\)

    Hint: Consider \(g=f+f',\) \(h=f-f'\)
